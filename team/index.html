How Legara Works

The operating model in plain language. Memorize the three pillars.

What We Are (and Are Not)

Legara is a regulated workforce platform. We build and manage a behavioral health operating infrastructure that runs alongside an FQHC's existing team. We handle the operational layer (scheduling, credentialing, compliance, provider support) so their providers can focus on patients.

Never call us: a staffing company, a telehealth company, or an outsourcing firm. These are fundamentally different models. Legara is a managed operational infrastructure.

FQHCs need behavioral health capacity, but they're structurally constrained. California employment law, HRSA requirements, union contracts, and primary care operating structures create a ceiling on what's possible. Providers share scheduling with primary care, dental, and everything else. There's no dedicated behavioral health infrastructure. That ceiling is real and it's not anyone's fault. Legara exists to add capacity through a different structure that sits alongside the FQHC's own team.

The Three Pillars

Everything we do is built on three structural advantages. These are the answer to "why is Legara different?"

1. Dedicated Infrastructure

Every Legara provider has a dedicated Patient Service Rep (PSR) who handles only behavioral health patients at strict ratios. No shared front desk. No splitting time with primary care. These ratios are empirically validated: loosen the ratio and productivity drops immediately; restore it and it returns.

PSRs handle scheduling, same-day backfill for cancellations, follow-up cadence, and crisis slot management. The FQHC approves the schedule and availability; Legara handles patient-level booking.

2. Incentive Alignment

Legara providers are compensated per encounter, not salaried. Every empty time block impacts their earnings directly. The provider pays the MSO (Legara Servicing) for operational support. This creates self-reinforcing utilization: every actor in the system is aligned toward keeping providers productive.

Note: The per-encounter structure is effective but can raise questions. In BD conversations, reference it briefly ("incentive-aligned providers") but don't go deep on the mechanics. Roger handles this in meetings.

3. Quality & Compliance

Documentation must be completed and the encounter locked in the EHR before billing is sent. Every claim is scrubbed daily for accuracy before it reaches the FQHC billing office, catching coding errors, diagnosis mismatches, and place-of-service issues.

Monthly quality check: random 3% sampling of every provider's notes, verified for uniqueness and appropriate length. Largely automated with manual safety checks where they matter.

The Legara Platform includes a secure portal with ambient AI notetaking. Patient-consented, private encryption keys, not running on a public LLM. This eliminates the productivity-vs-documentation tradeoff.

The Core Insight

The same clinician, with a dedicated operations layer around them, produces fundamentally different throughput. Not by working harder. By having the right support. A Legara PMHNP completes 2.5 encounters per hour. A typical FQHC PMHNP completes 1.5. That's not a quality difference. That's an infrastructure difference.

What the FQHC Gets

  • Behavioral health capacity without FTE risk or salary burn
  • Providers seeing patients in as few as 6 weeks from signed contract
  • Zero cost during the credentialing and panel-build period
  • Under 3% annual provider turnover (vs 30%+ industry average)
  • Providers document in the FQHC's own EHR, same day
  • Daily claims scrubbing before it hits the FQHC billing office
  • Revenue from completed encounters (PPS + copays + secondaries) averages ~$230/encounter
  • Net "mission cash generated" after paying Legara: real dollars the FQHC keeps for its mission

What Legara Servicing Does (Invisible to Client)

Legara Servicing is the Management Services Organization (MSO). It handles all provider operations: scheduling, credentialing management, compensation, quality monitoring, platform support, and compliance infrastructure. The FQHC never interacts with the MSO directly. To them, Legara providers simply integrate into their care team.

Never mention Legara Servicing or the MSO to clients. This is internal infrastructure. The client sees "Legara" as one entity that provides the full solution.

Sales Process

How a prospect becomes a client. Five journeys, one narrative arc.

Every prospect interaction, regardless of the journey, follows this four-stage arc. The order matters enormously.

Stage 1: Recognition

"Behavioral health demand outgrew the operating model it runs inside. That's structural, not effort."

The prospect needs to recognize that their BH capacity constraint is structural, not a staffing or management failure. This is the empathy frame. We never shame.

Stage 2: Understanding

"Same providers, different structure, different outcomes. Here's what that structure looks like."

The three pillars. Dedicated infrastructure. Incentive alignment. Quality safeguards. This is where the prospect learns what makes Legara fundamentally different from hiring more staff.

Stage 3: Proof

"50,000+ encounters per year. 9 FQHC partners. Specific, verifiable outcomes."

Concrete metrics: encounter volume, turnover rates, wait time reduction, utilization numbers. This stage makes the structural story credible.

Stage 4: Financial Validation

"Here's what it costs and why. The infrastructure is built into the rate."

Rates, mission cash, true cost comparison. This stage validates the decision after the prospect already understands and believes in the model.

Critical rule: Stage 4 must NEVER arrive before Stage 2. If a prospect hits price before they understand the operating model, the flat rate will always feel expensive compared to what they think they pay for FTEs. This is the "conditioning gap" and it's the #1 reason deals stall.

ABM Playbook

How Roger runs daily outreach, and how you can adapt it for your prospects.

The Philosophy

Legara's outreach is CEO-to-CEO. It is not a marketing campaign. It is one operator reaching out to another because he believes what he built solves a real problem they're dealing with right now.

The Friday Night Test: Would a health center CEO believe another CEO actually wrote this at 6 PM on a Friday? If it reads like a template, a sales team, or a marketing automation, it fails.

Why This Approach Works

FQHCs are inundated with staffing pitches, telehealth vendor emails, and healthcare marketing. Most of it is obviously templated and comes from sales reps. Legara's outreach works because:

  • Peer-to-peer positioning. Roger is a CEO writing to a CEO. Not a sales rep. Not a marketing team. The subject lines, tone, and content reflect this.
  • Structural differentiation leads. We don't open with cost savings or a product pitch. We open with an observation about their structural BH capacity constraint, specific to their organization.
  • Personalization is real. Every email opens with something specific that shows someone actually looked. An active job posting, a recent expansion, rural geography challenges.
  • Multi-stakeholder coverage. Same org, same day, different angles for different roles. CEO gets the structural capacity angle. CFO gets risk elimination. COO gets zero operational burden. CMO gets clinical integration.
  • The follow-up earns attention. Touch 2 adds value (different angle, different resource). Touch 3 shifts channel (LinkedIn, phone). Touch 4+ stays visible without annoying. We stop after consistent zero engagement.

Daily Structure

Volume

8-12 orgs per day. 2-4 contacts per org. Total: 20-40 emails daily.

Batch Composition

3-4 orgs getting Touch 1 (first contact). 2-3 orgs getting Touch 2 (follow-up, 3-5 days later). 1-2 orgs getting Touch 3 (channel shift). 1-2 re-engagements (OOO returns, bounced contacts).

Touch Sequence

Touch 1: The Introduction

120-180 words. Never over 200. Structure:

  • Personalization line (1 sentence, specific to them)
  • Who Roger is (1 sentence)
  • Why he's reaching out to them specifically (2-3 sentences connecting their situation to Legara)
  • One proof point (9 partners, 950+ encounters/week, or a relevant metric)
  • One link (GoLegara.com/assessment for CEO/BH Director/HR, GoLegara.com/next for COO/CMO)
  • Soft close (invitation, not ask)

Touch 2: The Follow-Up (Day 3-5)

Under 100 words. Reply to same thread. Different angle or resource than Touch 1. No re-explanation of what Legara is. Rotate the link: if Touch 1 linked to assessment, Touch 2 links to How It Works, and vice versa.

Touch 3: Channel Shift (Day 8-10)

Try a different channel. LinkedIn connection request (pre-warm by engaging with their posts first), InMail, or phone call for high-priority targets.

Touch 4+: Long Game (Day 14+)

One touch every 2-3 weeks. Share industry data, reference their LinkedIn activity, or mention a new proof point. After Touch 4 with zero engagement (no opens, no clicks, no LinkedIn views), move to Brevo Sequence C (monthly automated nurture).

Objection Handling

The objections you'll hear and how to address them. These are drawn from real conversations.

"That rate seems expensive. We can hire a therapist for $135K."

This is the conditioning gap. They're comparing Legara's all-in encounter rate to a salary number that excludes benefits (1.4x), support staff ($11K/yr), recruiter fees ($8K), ramp cost (6-9 months of salary before full caseload), and turnover replacement (30%/yr). The true loaded cost of that $135K therapist is approximately $229K/yr. But the real answer isn't the math. It's: "Let's back up. The rate comparison makes more sense once you see what the operating structure produces. Can I walk you through how the model works?"

If they persist on price before understanding the model: Offer to walk through the numbers together (the calculator, live, with Roger). Never send the calculator link. The conversation around the numbers is what makes the comparison land.

"We already have a telehealth vendor."

Legara isn't telehealth. Telehealth is a delivery mechanism. Legara is an operating infrastructure. Our providers may deliver care via telehealth, but they credential at the FQHC's sites, document in their EHR, coordinate with their primary care team, and operate within a managed quality system. The question isn't "do you have telehealth?" It's "does your current model give you dedicated BH scheduling, incentive-aligned utilization, and daily claims quality monitoring?"

"We tried something like this before and it didn't work."

Ask what they tried. Almost always it was a staffing company (no operational layer) or a telehealth platform (no integration). The difference is structural: Legara provides the full infrastructure around the provider, not just the provider. Dedicated scheduling, quality monitoring, documentation support, turnover management. The question is what was missing from the previous attempt, and we can show how Legara's three-pillar structure addresses those specific gaps.

"Our board won't approve an external vendor for clinical services."

Legara providers credential at the FQHC's sites. Clinical oversight stays with the FQHC's medical director. The FQHC approves the schedule, the provider types, and the clinical protocols. Legara handles the operational layer. This is augmentation of their existing program, not outsourcing clinical control. Frame it as: "Your team maintains all clinical governance. We handle the operations that make providers productive."

"We're in the middle of a hiring cycle already."

That's fine. Legara doesn't compete with their internal recruiting. We augment alongside it. If they're hiring one therapist, Legara can add two more while that hire ramps up. When the internal hire is fully productive (6-9 months out), they have more total capacity, not a conflict. The FQHC can always adjust Legara's provider count up or down based on their internal staffing trajectory.

"What happens if a Legara provider leaves?"

Legara manages replacement. Under 3% annual turnover vs 30%+ industry average. If a provider does leave, Legara handles recruiting, credentialing, and placement of the replacement. The FQHC doesn't restart their own hiring cycle. That's one of the core structural advantages: turnover risk is Legara's to manage, not the FQHC's.

How We Got Here

The evolution of Legara's positioning and why it matters. Read this if you want the full context behind today's messaging.

The Origin

Legara is not a startup. It's built on a decade of behavioral health operations with FQHC partners in California. The founding team (Roger Stellers, Bill Wells, Roy Rodriguez) spent 10 years together building this specific model as the outcome of working directly with FQHC partners. The model itself is what a decade of FQHC partnership produced.

Today, Legara serves 9 FQHC partners across California, delivering over 50,000 patient encounters per year. The company is profitable and debt-free.

The Messaging Evolution (Why It Matters to You)

If you've been getting updates from Roger over the past few months, you may have heard different versions of the story. That's because the positioning has evolved based on real market feedback. Here's the arc:

Phase 1: Financial-First (January-Early March 2026)

Early positioning led with cost comparison. "Your therapist costs $229K. Legara costs $155/encounter." The ROI calculator was the primary conversion tool. This was logical but produced a problem: prospects who reached the price conversation before understanding the operating model experienced sticker shock. They compared Legara's rate to what they thought they paid ($135K salary), not what they actually paid ($229K loaded). The calculator generated zero conversions.

Phase 2: The Conditioning Gap Discovery (March 2026)

Roger identified the core problem: the "conditioning gap." Prospects reaching the financial conversation without first understanding what Legara's operating structure produces, and why it's fundamentally different from hiring, would always perceive the rate as expensive. The worst outcome was real: deploying successfully but the client still viewing Legara as "an expensive crisis vendor" because the operational story never landed.

Phase 3: Structural Differentiation-First (Current)

The pivot: lead with structural differentiation, not financial comparison. The narrative arc (Recognition → Understanding → Proof → Financial Validation) ensures that prospects understand the operating model before they encounter the rate. The BH Capacity Assessment replaced the ROI calculator as the primary conversion tool. The calculator still exists as a selective tool Roger uses in live CFO walkthroughs.

Financial messaging is deemphasized, not eliminated. "Mission cash generated" remains the vocabulary. The order changed (4th not 1st), not the content.

Phase 4: Timeline Corrections (Late March 2026)

Two claims that had been in use were corrected after independent verification:

  • Legara provider placement: "5 months from contract" was corrected to "as fast as 6 weeks from signed contract." The old number included pre-contract negotiation time.
  • Internal hire ramp: "11 months" was corrected to "6-9 months from hire to full caseload." The old number used sequential math that ignored concurrent steps (credentialing and payer enrollment run in parallel) and included recruiting time. The corrected range is defensible and verified against published AAPPR benchmarking data.

The Empathy Frame

One of the most important developments: how we talk about the FQHC's current situation. FQHCs run behavioral health inside a primary care operating structure not by choice, but by structural necessity. California employment law, HRSA requirements, union contracts all constrain how they can structure BH operations. The ceiling is real and it's not a management failure.

We never shame. We never imply the prospect's model is broken or that they're doing it wrong. The constraint is structural. Legara exists to add capacity through a different structure that sits alongside what they already have. This frame is non-negotiable across every touchpoint.

Why This Matters for BD

Understanding this evolution protects you from two mistakes: (1) using outdated messaging that's been deliberately retired, and (2) leading with financial arguments before the structural story has landed. Both have cost real deals.

When this portal updates, you'll see the changes in the "What Changed" section at the top. Everything on this portal is the current approved messaging. If it's not here, it's not approved.
