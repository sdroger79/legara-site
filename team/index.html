How Legara Works

The operating model in plain language. The three pillars are the core of every conversation.

What We Are (and Are Not)

Legara is a regulated workforce platform. We build and manage a behavioral health operating infrastructure that runs alongside an FQHC's existing team. We handle the operational layer (scheduling, credentialing, compliance, provider support) so their providers can focus on patients.

We avoid: staffing company, telehealth company, outsourcing. Each puts us in a box with a negative association. Legara is a managed operational infrastructure.

FQHCs need behavioral health capacity, but they're structurally constrained. California employment law, HRSA requirements, union contracts, and primary care operating structures create a ceiling on what's possible. Providers share scheduling with primary care, dental, and everything else. There's no dedicated behavioral health infrastructure. That ceiling is real and it's not anyone's fault. Legara exists to add capacity through a different structure that sits alongside the FQHC's own team.

The Three Pillars

Everything we do is built on three structural advantages. These are the answer to "why is Legara different?"

1. Dedicated Infrastructure

Every Legara provider has a dedicated Patient Service Rep (PSR) who handles only behavioral health patients at strict ratios. No shared front desk. No splitting time with primary care. These ratios are empirically validated: loosen the ratio and productivity drops immediately; restore it and it returns.

PSRs handle scheduling, same-day backfill for cancellations, follow-up cadence, and crisis slot management. The FQHC approves the schedule and availability; Legara handles patient-level booking.

2. Incentive Alignment

Legara providers are compensated per encounter, not salaried. Every empty time block impacts their earnings directly. Every actor in the system is aligned toward keeping providers productive. This is why utilization numbers are so much higher than what FQHCs see with salaried staff.

In prospect conversations, you can reference "incentive-aligned providers" but the short version is enough for most conversations: "Our providers are incentivized to stay productive, which is why the utilization numbers are what they are." If a prospect wants to go deeper on compensation structure, Roger can cover that in the meeting.

3. Quality & Compliance

Documentation must be completed and the encounter locked in the EHR before billing is sent. Every claim is scrubbed daily for accuracy before it reaches the FQHC billing office, catching coding errors, diagnosis mismatches, and place-of-service issues.

Monthly quality check: random 3% sampling of every provider's notes, verified for uniqueness and appropriate length. Largely automated with manual safety checks where they matter.

The Legara Platform includes a secure portal with ambient AI notetaking. Patient-consented, private encryption keys, not running on a public LLM. This eliminates the productivity-vs-documentation tradeoff.

The Core Insight

The same clinician, with a dedicated operations layer around them, produces fundamentally different throughput. Not by working harder. By having the right support. A Legara PMHNP completes 2.5 encounters per hour. A typical FQHC PMHNP completes 1.5. That's not a quality difference. That's an infrastructure difference.

What the FQHC Gets

  • Behavioral health capacity without FTE risk or salary burn
  • Providers seeing patients in as few as 6 weeks from signed contract
  • Zero cost during the credentialing and panel-build period
  • Under 3% annual provider turnover (vs 30%+ industry average)
  • Providers document in the FQHC's own EHR, same day
  • Daily claims scrubbing before it hits the FQHC billing office
  • Revenue from completed encounters (PPS + copays + secondaries) averages ~$230/encounter
  • Net cash generated to serve the mission after paying Legara: real dollars the FQHC keeps

One thing to know: There is a separate internal entity called Legara Servicing that handles the operational backend. Clients never see or interact with it. If a prospect asks about corporate structure, "Legara handles everything on the operational side" covers it. The internal entity details are not relevant to the client relationship.

Other Comparisons You May Encounter

Beyond the three main comparisons above, prospects sometimes reference these alternatives.

vs. Locum Tenens

Locums are temporary. Legara is a permanent infrastructure. Locums fill gaps for weeks or months. They don't come with scheduling support, quality monitoring, documentation infrastructure, or turnover management. Locum rates are often higher than Legara's flat rate, and the FQHC gets none of the operational benefits. The comparison: locums are a band-aid. Legara is a managed program.

vs. Academic / Trainee Programs

Training programs are valuable but they produce supervisory burden, not immediate capacity. Trainees need oversight, limited caseloads, and extended ramp time. Legara provides fully credentialed, experienced providers who are productive from day one. The two models can coexist: Legara handles volume while the training program develops the next generation.

vs. Referral Out (County BH, Community Agencies)

Referring patients out means losing continuity. The patient leaves the FQHC ecosystem, follow-up is inconsistent, and the FQHC doesn't capture the encounter revenue. Legara keeps patients inside the FQHC's system. Providers credential at their sites, document in their EHR, and coordinate with their primary care team. Revenue stays with the FQHC.

Stage 1: Recognition

"Behavioral health demand outgrew the operating model it runs inside. That's structural, not effort."

The prospect needs to recognize that their BH capacity constraint is structural, not a staffing or management failure. This is the empathy frame. We never shame.

Stage 2: Understanding

"Same providers, different structure, different outcomes. Here's what that structure looks like."

The three pillars. Dedicated infrastructure. Incentive alignment. Quality safeguards. This is where the prospect learns what makes Legara fundamentally different from hiring more staff.

Stage 3: Proof

"50,000+ encounters per year. 9 FQHC partners. Specific, verifiable outcomes."

Concrete metrics: encounter volume, turnover rates, wait time reduction, utilization numbers. This stage makes the structural story credible.

Stage 4: Financial Validation

"Here's what it costs and why. The infrastructure is built into the rate."

Rates, cash generated to serve the mission, true cost comparison. This stage validates the decision after the prospect already understands and believes in the model.

Worth knowing: When the financial conversation happens before the operating model conversation, deals tend to stall. Prospects compare the flat rate to a salary number and it feels expensive. Once they understand the three-pillar structure, the rate makes sense. This is the biggest lesson from the last year of sales conversations.

ABM Playbook

This is how I run my daily outreach and why I think it works. Take whatever is useful and make it your own.

The Philosophy

Legara's outreach is CEO-to-CEO. It is not a marketing campaign. It is one operator reaching out to another because he believes what he built solves a real problem they're dealing with right now.

The Friday Night Test: Would a health center CEO believe another CEO actually wrote this at 6 PM on a Friday? If it reads like a template, a sales team, or a marketing automation, it fails.

Why This Approach Works

After running this for a few weeks across about 125 emails, here is what I have noticed actually moves the needle:

  • Peer-to-peer positioning. I write CEO-to-CEO, not sales rep to prospect. The subject lines, tone, and content reflect that.
  • Structural differentiation leads. I never open with cost savings or a product pitch. I open with an observation about their structural behavioral health capacity constraint, specific to their organization.
  • Personalization is real. Every email opens with something specific I actually found about their organization. An active job posting, a recent expansion, rural geography challenges.
  • Multi-stakeholder coverage. Same org, same day, different angles for different roles. CEO gets the structural capacity angle. CFO gets risk elimination. COO gets zero operational burden. CMO gets clinical integration.
  • The follow-up earns attention. Touch 2 adds value (different angle, different resource). Touch 3 shifts channel (LinkedIn, phone). Touch 4+ stays visible without annoying. We stop after consistent zero engagement.

Daily Structure

Volume

I typically hit 8-12 orgs per day, 2-4 contacts per org. Total: 20-40 emails daily. Your volume will depend on your pipeline. This is what I have found is sustainable without the emails getting sloppy.

Batch Composition

3-4 orgs getting Touch 1 (first contact). 2-3 orgs getting Touch 2 (follow-up, 3-5 days later). 1-2 orgs getting Touch 3 (channel shift). 1-2 re-engagements (OOO returns, bounced contacts).

Touch Sequence

Touch 1: The Introduction

I keep these under 200 words. Anything longer and it reads like a pitch deck. The structure I have settled on:

  • Personalization line (1 sentence, specific to them)
  • Who Roger is (1 sentence)
  • Why he's reaching out to them specifically (2-3 sentences connecting their situation to Legara)
  • One proof point (9 partners, 950+ encounters/week, or a relevant metric)
  • One link (GoLegara.com/assessment for CEO/BH Director/HR, GoLegara.com/next for COO/CMO)
  • Soft close (invitation, not ask)

Touch 2: The Follow-Up (Day 3-5)

Under 100 words. Reply to same thread. Different angle or resource than Touch 1. No re-explanation of what Legara is. Rotate the link: if Touch 1 linked to assessment, Touch 2 links to How It Works, and vice versa.

Touch 3: Channel Shift (Day 8-10)

Try a different channel. LinkedIn connection request (pre-warm by engaging with their posts first), InMail, or phone call for high-priority targets.

Touch 4+: Long Game (Day 14+)

One touch every 2-3 weeks. Share industry data, reference their LinkedIn activity, or mention a new proof point. After Touch 4 with zero engagement (no opens, no clicks, no LinkedIn views), stop manual outreach. They'll enter an automated monthly nurture that keeps Legara visible without requiring your effort.

What Happens After Your Outreach

Once a prospect takes the assessment, they automatically receive a series of follow-up emails over the next two weeks that educate them on the three-pillar model. You don't need to send these. They're handled by the system.

If the prospect books a meeting at any point, the automated emails stop and they receive a meeting confirmation instead. If they complete the automated series without booking, they enter a long-term monthly nurture that keeps Legara on their radar. The heavy lifting is the initial outreach and any personal follow-ups. Everything after the assessment is automated.

"That rate seems expensive. We can hire a therapist for $135K."

This is the conditioning gap. They're comparing Legara's all-in encounter rate to a salary number that excludes benefits (1.4x), support staff ($11K/yr), recruiter fees ($8K), ramp cost (6-9 months of salary before full caseload), and turnover replacement (30%/yr). The true loaded cost of that $135K therapist is approximately $229K/yr. But the real answer isn't the math. It's: "Let's back up. The rate comparison makes more sense once you see what the operating structure produces. Can I walk you through how the model works?"

If they persist on price before understanding the model: Offer to walk through the numbers together (the calculator, live, with Roger). I don't send the calculator link. The conversation around the numbers is what makes the comparison land. If a CFO wants to see numbers, I walk them through it live.

"We already have a telehealth vendor."

Legara isn't telehealth. Telehealth is a delivery mechanism. Legara is an operating infrastructure. Our providers may deliver care via telehealth, but they credential at the FQHC's sites, document in their EHR, coordinate with their primary care team, and operate within a managed quality system. The question isn't "do you have telehealth?" It's "does your current model give you dedicated BH scheduling, incentive-aligned utilization, and daily claims quality monitoring?"

"We tried something like this before and it didn't work."

Ask what they tried. Almost always it was a staffing company (no operational layer) or a telehealth platform (no integration). The difference is structural: Legara provides the full infrastructure around the provider, not just the provider. Dedicated scheduling, quality monitoring, documentation support, turnover management. The question is what was missing from the previous attempt, and we can show how Legara's three-pillar structure addresses those specific gaps.

"Our board won't approve an external vendor for clinical services."

Legara providers credential at the FQHC's sites. Clinical oversight stays with the FQHC's medical director. The FQHC approves the schedule, the provider types, and the clinical protocols. Legara handles the operational layer. This is augmentation of their existing program, not outsourcing clinical control. Frame it as: "Your team maintains all clinical governance. We handle the operations that make providers productive."

"We're in the middle of a hiring cycle already."

That's fine. Legara doesn't compete with their internal recruiting. We augment alongside it. If they're hiring one therapist, Legara can add two more while that hire ramps up. When the internal hire is fully productive (6-9 months out), they have more total capacity, not a conflict. The FQHC can always adjust Legara's provider count up or down based on their internal staffing trajectory.

"What happens if a Legara provider leaves?"

Legara manages replacement. Under 3% annual turnover vs 30%+ industry average. If a provider does leave, Legara handles recruiting, credentialing, and placement of the replacement. The FQHC doesn't restart their own hiring cycle. That's one of the core structural advantages: turnover risk is Legara's to manage, not the FQHC's.

"We're too busy / no bandwidth right now."

What they really mean: Adding a new vendor feels like more work, not less.

This is where the operational model matters most. Legara doesn't create work for the FQHC. We handle scheduling, credentialing, compliance, documentation support, and quality monitoring. The FQHC's ops team approves the provider's schedule parameters. After that, Legara handles everything at the patient level. The total lift on the FQHC side is less than onboarding a new hire.

"Will my board approve this? How do I describe it to them?"

What they really mean: I need to sell this internally and I'm not sure how to frame it.

Frame it as augmentation of existing behavioral health capacity through a managed services model. The FQHC retains all clinical governance. No FTE risk. Variable cost tied to completed encounters. Under 3% provider turnover. If the board needs a formal overview, Roger can provide a brief presentation tailored to their governance questions. The framing that tends to work: "We're adding capacity alongside our existing team through a managed model that eliminates the hiring and ramp risk."

"I can't manage more contractors."

What they really mean: I've been burned by contractors who need babysitting.

Legara providers are not contractors the BH Director manages. They come with their own operational support layer: dedicated scheduling, documentation infrastructure, quality monitoring, and compliance oversight. The BH Director's role is clinical coordination, not contractor management. If a provider isn't performing, that's Legara's problem to solve, not the BH Director's.

"Remote clinicians won't fit our population."

What they really mean: Our patients have language, cultural, or acuity needs that require specific provider matching.

Provider matching is part of Legara's model. We credential providers who match the FQHC's population needs: language capabilities, cultural competency, specialty focus, comfort with acuity levels. The FQHC approves every provider before they see patients. If a provider isn't the right fit, Legara handles the replacement. This is a conversation to have in the clinical alignment meeting with specific population details on the table.

"Quality will be inconsistent."

What they really mean: I've seen quality slip when providers aren't embedded in the team.

Legara's quality infrastructure is more structured than what most FQHCs run internally. Daily claims scrubbing catches coding and documentation issues before they reach the billing office. Monthly 3% random sampling of every provider's notes, verified for uniqueness and appropriate length. Documentation must be completed and the encounter locked in the EHR same day. The BH Director has visibility into all of this.

"Our workflows are fragile. Don't break them."

What they really mean: We've spent years getting things to work. Any disruption is unacceptable.

Legara providers credential at the FQHC's sites and document in the FQHC's EHR. From a workflow perspective, they look and act like internal providers. The key difference is who handles their scheduling and operational support. The clinical alignment meeting maps this out in detail: referral pathways, EHR documentation standards, communication protocols, warm handoff procedures. Nothing changes in the FQHC's existing workflows.

"I'm taking on clinical risk without control."

What they really mean: If something goes wrong clinically, it falls on me.

Clinical governance stays with the FQHC. The Medical Director or CMO retains authority over clinical protocols, prescribing guidelines, and escalation procedures. Legara handles the operational layer (scheduling, documentation support, compliance monitoring), not the clinical layer. The boundaries are explicit and documented in the agreement. If the Medical Director wants to review a provider's clinical approach, they have full access.

"How is supervision handled, especially for PMHNPs?"

What they really mean: I need to understand the legal supervision structure before I'm comfortable.

Legara provides physician supervision for PMHNPs through our own supervising physicians. The supervision model meets California's standardized procedure requirements. The FQHC's Medical Director doesn't take on additional supervision burden. Legara handles the supervision documentation and compliance. If the Medical Director wants to be involved in the supervision framework or review the standardized procedures, that's welcomed and built into the clinical alignment process.

"Remote prescribing concerns me."

What they really mean: Medication management via telehealth feels risky.

Legara PMHNPs follow the FQHC's prescribing guidelines and formulary. All prescribing is documented in the FQHC's EHR with full visibility to the care team. The supervision model includes medication management oversight. The FQHC sets the clinical boundaries. If there are specific controlled substance policies, acuity limits, or prescribing protocols, those are established during clinical alignment and Legara providers follow them.

"Who bills? Are you billing for us?"

What they really mean: I need to understand the revenue cycle mechanics.

The FQHC bills. Legara providers document in the FQHC's EHR and complete all documentation requirements for billing. Legara's daily claims scrub reviews every encounter for coding accuracy, diagnosis consistency, and place-of-service correctness before it reaches the FQHC's billing office. The FQHC submits claims, collects revenue (PPS + copays + secondaries), and pays Legara's flat per-encounter rate. The difference is cash generated to serve the mission.

"How do payments and reconciliation work?"

What they really mean: I need to see the financial mechanics before I can sign off.

Legara provides monthly settlement statements showing every completed encounter by provider, date, and CPT code. The FQHC verifies against their own EHR records. Payment is the flat per-encounter rate times completed encounters. No hidden fees, no platform charges, no add-ons. The rate is truly all-in. Jonathon handles the detailed financial walkthrough for prospects at this stage.

"What about liability and insurance?"

What they really mean: If something goes wrong, who's exposed?

Legara carries its own professional liability and general liability insurance. Legara providers are covered under Legara's policies, not the FQHC's. The agreement includes standard indemnification provisions. The FQHC is not taking on additional liability for Legara providers beyond what's normal for credentialed providers at their sites. Jonathon and the compliance team handle the specifics during the contracting process.

Guardrails

A few things worth being intentional about in prospect conversations.

How We Frame Ourselves

Legara is a regulated workforce platform. Not a staffing company, not a telehealth vendor, not outsourcing. These distinctions matter because prospects will try to put us in a familiar box, and every one of those boxes has a negative association. Staffing means "you send a body." Telehealth means "a platform, not real integration." Outsourcing means "you're replacing our people."

The right framing: Legara builds and manages the operational infrastructure for behavioral health alongside the FQHC's existing team. We augment. We never replace.

How We Talk About Their Situation

This is the most important guardrail. FQHCs run behavioral health inside a primary care operating structure because they have to. California employment law, HRSA requirements, union contracts. The ceiling is structural. It is not a management failure, and we never imply that it is.

The instinct to point out problems is natural, but it backfires. These leaders already know their behavioral health program is stretched. What they need is someone who understands why and has an answer, not someone explaining the problem back to them. The framing is always: "the constraint is structural, not effort" or "the operating model has a ceiling that no amount of hiring fixes."

How We Talk About Money

Financial validation supports the sale. It never leads. The phrase is "cash generated to serve your mission," never "savings" or "cost savings." The difference matters: savings implies the FQHC is overspending. Cash generated to serve the mission implies they're generating new revenue with more leftover after Legara's expense. One frames them as inefficient. The other frames them as resourceful.

On specific rates: keep it directional in early conversations. "A flat per-encounter rate, all-in" is enough. Roger handles detailed pricing walkthroughs. If a prospect presses on numbers before they understand the model, the move is to pull back: "The numbers make a lot more sense once you see what the operating structure produces. Let me walk you through that first."

How We Got Here

The evolution of Legara's positioning and why it matters. Read this if you want the full context behind today's messaging.

The Origin

Legara is not a startup. It's built on a decade of behavioral health operations with FQHC partners in California. The founding team (Roger Stellers, Bill Wells, Roy Rodriguez) spent 10 years together building this specific model as the outcome of working directly with FQHC partners. The model itself is what a decade of FQHC partnership produced.

Today, Legara serves 9 FQHC partners across California, delivering over 50,000 patient encounters per year. The company is profitable and debt-free.

The Messaging Evolution (Why It Matters to You)

If you've been getting updates from Roger over the past few months, you may have heard different versions of the story. That's because the positioning has evolved based on real market feedback. Here's the arc:

Phase 1: Financial-First (January-Early March 2026)

Early positioning led with cost comparison. "Your therapist costs $229K. Legara costs $155/encounter." The ROI calculator was the primary conversion tool. This was logical but produced a problem: prospects who reached the price conversation before understanding the operating model experienced sticker shock. They compared Legara's rate to what they thought they paid ($135K salary), not what they actually paid ($229K loaded). The calculator generated zero conversions.

Phase 2: The Conditioning Gap Discovery (March 2026)

Roger identified the core problem: the "conditioning gap." Prospects reaching the financial conversation without first understanding what Legara's operating structure produces, and why it's fundamentally different from hiring, would always perceive the rate as expensive. The worst outcome was real: deploying successfully but the client still viewing Legara as "an expensive crisis vendor" because the operational story never landed.

Phase 3: Structural Differentiation-First (Current)

The pivot: lead with structural differentiation, not financial comparison. The narrative arc (Recognition → Understanding → Proof → Financial Validation) ensures that prospects understand the operating model before they encounter the rate. The BH Capacity Assessment replaced the ROI calculator as the primary conversion tool. The calculator still exists as a selective tool Roger uses in live CFO walkthroughs.

Financial messaging is deemphasized, not eliminated. "Cash generated to serve the mission" is the vocabulary. The order changed (4th not 1st), not the content.

Phase 4: Timeline Corrections (Late March 2026)

Two claims that had been in use were corrected after independent verification:

  • Legara provider placement: "5 months from contract" was corrected to "as fast as 6 weeks from signed contract." The old number included pre-contract negotiation time.
  • Internal hire ramp: "11 months" was corrected to "6-9 months from hire to full caseload." The old number used sequential math that ignored concurrent steps (credentialing and payer enrollment run in parallel) and included recruiting time. The corrected range is defensible and verified against published AAPPR benchmarking data.

The Empathy Frame

One of the most important developments: how we talk about the FQHC's current situation. FQHCs run behavioral health inside a primary care operating structure not by choice, but by structural necessity. California employment law, HRSA requirements, union contracts all constrain how they can structure BH operations. The ceiling is real and it's not a management failure.

We never shame. We never imply the prospect's model is broken or that they're doing it wrong. The constraint is structural. Legara exists to add capacity through a different structure that sits alongside what they already have. This frame is non-negotiable across every touchpoint.

Why This Matters for BD

Understanding this evolution protects you from two mistakes: (1) accidentally using old messaging that has been updated, and (2) leading with financial arguments before the structural story has landed. Both have tripped up real conversations.
